Unit 5: Wave

Mechanical Waves

Mechanical waves require a medium to propagate. They can be classified into transverse waves and longitudinal waves.

Transverse Waves

In transverse waves, particles move perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation.

Longitudinal Waves

In longitudinal waves, particles move parallel to the direction of wave propagation (vibrates in the same direction as the wave's motion).

Standing Waves

Standing waves are formed by the interference of two waves traveling in opposite directions. It stays in a constant position, and are the result of an interference between two waves travelling in different directions.

Interference

When two waves of the same type meet, they combine to create a larger or smaller wave.

Constructive Interference

Constructive interference occurs when two waves meet and are in phase, leading to a stronger resultant wave.

Antinode: Location of complete constructive interference. The wave will have the greatest displacement at an antinode.

Destructive Interference

Destructive interference occurs when two waves meet with opposite phase, leading to a weaker resultant wave or complete cancellation.

Node: Location of complete destructive interference between the incident (initial) wave and the refelcted wave. The wave does not move at a node.
