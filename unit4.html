Unit 4: Electricity and Magnetism

Electrostatics

Microstructure of Matter

Matter is made up of atoms, and atoms can be broken down into protons (postitve charge), electrons (negatively charge), and neutrons (no charge). Each atom has a core called nucleus that contains positively charged protons and uncharged neutrons. It's surrounded by a "cloud" of smaller negatively charged electrons. These electrons orbit the nucleus in energy levels. Electrons must asorb energy in order to move to a higher level. When the electron falls to a lower energy level, it releases energy. The elementary charge, e, is equal in magnatude to the charge on a electron (-e) or the charge on a proton (+e). Neutral atoms will have a equal number of protons and electrons and have a net charge of 0.

Charged Objects

An atom will become an ion when they gain or lose electrons. If a atom loses one or more electron it has a net postitve charge, and will be a positive ion. If a atom gains one or more electron it has a net negative charge, and will be a negative ion. Like charges will repel each other while opposite charges attract each other. The charge on an object is represented as the symbol q.

Transfer of Charge

When a system has only neutral objects it has a total net charge of 0. When objects in the system are rubbed together, electrons can be transfered between the objects.

Law of Conservation of Charge

Charge is always conserved. The total amount of charge and the amount of charge among the objects is the same before and after the process starts and ends

Quantity of Charge

The electric charge, q, is a scalar quantity. Charge is a fundamental measurement with a unit of Coulomb [C]. The magnitude of charge on a single proton or electron is called elementary charge (e), which is 1.6x10-19 coulomb. To make up one single coulomb of charge it would take 6.25x1018 elementary charges. The net charge of a charged object is always a multiple of e, which means the charge is quantized.

Coulomb's Law

Fe = kq1q2 / r2

Fe = Electrostatic force (N), q1 and q2 = charges in coulombs (C), k Electrostatic force (8.99x109 N*m2/C2), r = distance of separation (m)
